Monsoon Dynamics and Intraseasonal Oscillations

The Asian summer monsoon exhibits pronounced variability on 20–90-day timescales, commonly referred to as the Monsoon Intraseasonal Oscillation (MISO). These oscillations regulate the transition between active and break phases of the monsoon and strongly influence extreme rainfall, agriculture, and water resources over South Asia. Understanding the mechanisms governing the initiation, northward propagation, and predictability of MISO has been a central focus of my research.

My work has contributed to understanding the dynamics of northward propagation using observational analyses, vorticity budgets, moist static energy diagnostics, and regional coupled atmosphere–ocean models. More recently, I have investigated how these physical mechanisms can be incorporated into process-oriented diagnostics for improving extended-range prediction systems.

Subseasonal-to-Seasonal (S2S) Climate Prediction

Accurate prediction of weather and climate at subseasonal-to-seasonal (S2S) timescales (2–8 weeks) remains one of the grand challenges in atmospheric science. This prediction window bridges short-range weather forecasting and seasonal climate outlooks, requiring an improved understanding of the interaction between synoptic disturbances, tropical intraseasonal oscillations, air–sea coupling, and slowly varying boundary conditions. Reliable S2S prediction has enormous societal benefits for agriculture, water-resource management, disaster preparedness, and energy planning.

My research focuses on improving S2S prediction through process-oriented diagnostics, multi-model ensemble forecasting, and physically interpretable machine learning approaches. I have investigated the representation of northward-propagating monsoon convection in operational forecast systems, evaluated regional coupled atmosphere–ocean models, and developed diagnostic frameworks based on moist static energy and vorticity budgets to identify systematic model biases. More recently, my work has expanded toward hybrid AI–physics prediction systems that integrate physical understanding with modern machine learning techniques to improve forecast skill and interpretability.

Monsoon Low Pressure Systems and Tropical Disturbances

Monsoon low-pressure systems (LPSs), including monsoon depressions, are among the most important synoptic-scale weather systems governing the distribution of rainfall over the Indian subcontinent. Their frequency, intensity, and propagation strongly influence seasonal monsoon rainfall and extreme weather events. Understanding the physical mechanisms responsible for their formation and evolution is therefore essential for improving weather and extended-range prediction.

My research investigates the interaction between synoptic-scale disturbances and larger-scale intraseasonal variability, with particular emphasis on the role of the Monsoon Intraseasonal Oscillation (MISO) in modulating the development of monsoon depressions. I have also contributed to improving the representation of northward-propagating convection in multi-model ensemble prediction systems and diagnosing model biases that affect the simulation of organized tropical convection.

Multidecadal Variability of the Indian Summer Monsoon

The Indian Summer Monsoon exhibits substantial variability not only from year to year but also on much longer, multidecadal timescales. Understanding the origin of these slow fluctuations is important for distinguishing natural climate variability from long-term climate change and for improving predictions of future monsoon behavior.

My research identified a dominant multidecadal mode of variability in the Indian Summer Monsoon Rainfall (ISMR) with a characteristic period of nearly seven decades using Multichannel Singular Spectrum Analysis (MSSA). The study demonstrated that this mode exerts a strong influence on the long-term occurrence of droughts and floods over India and represents a fundamental component of natural monsoon variability.

I further showed that this variability is closely linked to large-scale patterns of global sea surface temperature anomalies. The proposed mechanism connects slowly varying oceanic conditions over the Pacific and Atlantic Oceans to changes in atmospheric circulation over the northern Arabian Sea, ultimately modulating the strength of the monsoon circulation over India. These results provide new insights into the physical drivers of multidecadal monsoon variability and suggest a potential source of predictability at timescales beyond the seasonal cycle.
